## 5.4 Security Compliance
 
### Regulatory Compliance Frameworks
 
| Framework | Description |
|---|---|
| SOX | Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002). Public company accounting reform and investor protection. Financial reporting integrity requirements. |
| HIPAA | Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act. Healthcare standards for storage, use, and transmission of PHI. Spelled HIPAA, not HIPPA. |
| GLBA | Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (1999). Requires financial institutions to disclose privacy practices and protect customer financial data. |
| PCI DSS | Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard. Applies to any organization handling credit card data. Requires annual risk assessments, regular penetration testing, network segmentation, and encryption of cardholder data. |
| GDPR | General Data Protection Regulation. EU regulation. Applies to all EU citizen data regardless of where the company is headquartered. Right to be forgotten (right to erasure). Users decide where their data goes. |
 
### Consequences of Non-Compliance
 
- Fines: regulatory penalties based on violation severity and scope. HIPAA Class 4 (malicious use): up to $250,000 or 10 years.
- Sanctions: inability to operate in certain markets or sell products.
- Reputational damage: mandatory breach disclosure requirements expose non-compliance publicly.
- Loss of license: expensive and time-consuming to relicense. Customers cannot purchase from sanctioned companies.
- Contractual impacts: business deals require proof of compliance. Breach of compliance can void contracts.
### Compliance Monitoring
 
- Due diligence: act in good faith. Investigate and verify. Applied to third-party relationships.
- Due care: internal activities. Apply reasonable security measures to protect data and systems.
- Attestation/acknowledgment: formal approval of compliance documentation. The attestor is responsible for the accuracy of what they sign.
- Automation: third-party monitoring systems collect data from people and systems, compile reports, and surface compliance gaps continuously.
### Privacy
 
- GDPR: EU regulation covering name, address, email, bank details, medical information, IP address. Controls export of personal data. Right to be forgotten. Applies to all EU citizen data globally regardless of company location.
- Data subject: any individual whose personal data is collected. Laws and regulations are defined from the data subject's perspective.
- Data inventory: document all managed data including owner, update frequency, and format. Govern both internal use and external sharing. Comply with data retention laws.
